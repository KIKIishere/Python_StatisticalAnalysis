Traffic Accidents and Vehicle Involvement Between 2010 and 2011 in UK

Team Members & unique name: Muxue Liu, muxue; Yijia Wang, yijiaw, Qirong Wang, qirong.

Project Overview

This project aims to analyze traffic accidents and vehicle involvements between year 2010 and year 2011 to identify patterns and contributing factors. By combining two dataset together: accident information and vehicle information, conducting data manipulation and data visualization, we seek to understand the road accident better to improve the road safety.

Motivation

Traffic accidents lead to death, injuries and economic costs worldwide. Data driven insights can help us know more about the factors and thus prevent future incidents. Identifying high risk areas can help the policy makers pay more attention and take more actions, such as better road design. For another important factor, vehicles, this information can help manufactures improve the safety design and give some reference to customers of buying cars. We believe that these considerations will be meaningful to save more lives.

Data Sources

The data come from the Open Data website of the UK government, where they have been published by the Department of Transport. This data are also published on Kaggle, so that we use the dataset from Kaggle for investigation. We use two dataset: Accident_Information.csv, and Vehicle_Information.csv.(https://www.kaggle.com/datasets/tsiaras/uk-road-safety-accidents-and-vehicles?resource=download)

Accident information contains unique accidents, and the corresponding road information, location and weather condition features. Vehicle information contains unique vehicle conditions. They complement each other by providing a comprehensive view of each traffic incident, and can be linked by the unique traffic accident identifier.

Since the dataset comprises over 1.8 million records of traffic accidents, which is too big. We prefer to focus on analyzing between two years: year 2010 and year 2011.

Data Description

For the accident data, key variables include: Accident_Index: A unique identifier for each accident. Location: 1st and 2nd road class and number. Accident_Severity: Severity of the accident (e.g., Slight, Serious). Date and Time: When the accident occurred. Speed_limit: Speed limit of the road where the accident happened. Road_Type: Type of road (e.g., Single carriageway, Dual carriageway).

For the vehicle data, important variables are: Accident_Index: Linking the vehicle data to the accident data. Vehicle_Type: Type of vehicle involved. Vehicle_Manoeuvre: The maneuver the vehicle was performing at the time of the accident. Vehicle_Reference: A reference number for the vehicle involved in the accident. Data Manipulation

To combine these datasets, we'll use the Accident_Index as the common key. We'll perform an inner join to ensure we only include records that have corresponding entries in both datasets. This will allow us to analyze accidents with complete information about the vehicles involved.

Columns such as Accident_Index, 1st_Road_Class, Accident_Severity, Date, Day_of_Week, and several other columns have no missing data. These columns include identifiers, road classifications, accident severity levels, and temporal data.

The shape of the merged table is 2058408 rows and 57 columns. We specifically filtered out data for year 2010 and 2011, which is the most recent ones we can get in the dataset, then the table become 360983 rows and 57 columns.

Variables of Interest:

  1. Year_y we mainly focused on year 2010 and 2011.
  2. Accident_Severity categorical variable that set accident to three levels Slight which has 312066, Serious which has 44389 and Fatal which has 4528.
  3. Weather_Conditions categorical variable that split the weather into 10 different categories with "Fine no high winds" and "Raining no high winds" to be most common ones.
  4. Vehicle_Typecategorical variable that split car type into 15 different categories, with "Car" and "Van" to be the most common ones.

How did weather conditions impact the number of accidents in each year? Are there certain weather conditions more likely to be associated with accidents? Did the time of day or day of the week significantly affect the occurrence of accidents?

It is notable that, the weather condition factor that had the greatest impact on traffic accidents was "fine no high winds", with the number of accidents exceeding 140,000 in both years, and more in 2011 than in 2010. It is possible that fine weather conditions often lead to increased traffic volume as people are more inclined to travel. With more vehicles on the road, the likelihood of accidents naturally increases.It is also easier for the drivers to speed, distracted drive, or risky maneuvers， make it even worse to keep save on the road.

Then, this was closely followed by "raining no high winds", with more than 20,000 accidents, with the number in 2010 exceeding that in 2011 by a little. Rainfall creates wet road surfaces, reducing traction and increasing the likelihood of vehicles skidding or losing control, especially during the initial phase of rainfall when oil and debris on the road surface mix with water, making it particularly slippery. This can lead to an increase in the number of accidents during rainy conditions.Besides, weather patterns and rainfall levels can vary between years due to seasonal fluctuations and other climatic factors.

The minimal impact of "snowing and high winds" on traffic accidents, nearly zero, could be attributed to several factors. Firstly, individuals tend to avoid venturing out during severe weather conditions like heavy snowfall and high winds, resulting in fewer vehicles on the road and thus reducing the likelihood of accidents. Additionally, regions prone to such weather events often have robust infrastructure and road maintenance protocols in place to mitigate risks to road safety. Measures such as snowplowing, salting, and sanding help maintain road usability even during adverse weather, minimizing the potential for accidents. Moreover, heightened awareness among drivers regarding the hazards posed by snowing and high winds prompts them to take proactive measures, such as adjusting driving behavior and adhering to safety guidelines. This collective effort, encompassing both individual caution and systematic infrastructure improvements, contributes to the negligible impact of "snowing and high winds" on traffic accidents.

According to the plot, on Fridays there was a significant increase in crashes in both 2011 and 2010, however on Sundays crashes were the lowest number of the week.

The significant increase in crashes on Fridays, as observed in both 2011 and 2010, can be attributed to several factors related to human behavior and societal norms. Fridays are typically associated with the end of the work or school week in many cultures, leading to increased traffic volume as people commute home or travel for leisure activities. This surge in traffic combined with factors such as rush hour congestion, fatigue from a week of work or study, and heightened excitement for the upcoming weekend can contribute to a higher likelihood of accidents occurring.

Conversely, Sundays often see the lowest number of crashes for several reasons. Many individuals tend to stay home or engage in leisure activities closer to home on Sundays, resulting in reduced traffic volume compared to weekdays. Additionally, there may be fewer commercial activities and work-related travel on Sundays, further contributing to decreased congestion and a lower risk of accidents. Moreover, heightened awareness of the potential risks associated with weekend driving, coupled with a desire to relax and unwind before the start of the new week, may prompt drivers to exercise greater caution and adhere more strictly to traffic regulations on Sundays.

There were more traffic accidents in 2010 than in 2011 on Mondays and Tuesdays, but more accidents in 2011 than in 2010 on Wednesdays and Thursdays, could be influenced by economic and weather factors. For economic factors such as employment rates, consumer confidence, and business activities may vary between the two years. In 2010, following the global financial crisis, there might have been higher levels of economic uncertainty, leading to increased stress levels and potentially riskier driving behaviors at the beginning of the workweek. Conversely, by 2011, there could have been signs of economic recovery, resulting in improved job security and reduced financial stress for some individuals.For weather conditions can significantly impact driving conditions and accident rates. It's possible that in 2010, adverse weather events such as heavy rain, snow, or fog occurred more frequently on Mondays and Tuesdays, leading to an uptick in accidents. Conversely, in 2011, weather patterns may have been milder during the early weekdays, with adverse weather events occurring more frequently later in the week.

In 2011, much like in 2010, there was a noticeable correlation between the severity of crashes and the type of vehicle involved. Cars were the most prevalent vehicles involved in accidents. They were frequently associated with SLIGHT crashes, reflecting their high occurrence in minor incidents. However, cars also featured prominently in SERIOUS accidents, indicating their involvement in more severe collisions compared to other vehicle types.Additionally, cars are also among the types of vehicles involved in severe accidents. Despite safety features, accidents can still escalate to serious levels under certain circumstances, such as high-speed collisions or loss of control events.

Motorcycles with engine capacities exceeding 500cc and vans were more prone to being involved in serious accidents. The increased risk associated with these vehicles may stem from factors such as higher speeds, larger sizes, and potentially reduced maneuverability.

Surprisingly, no vehicle type other than a car was implicated in fatal accidents during the observed period. This suggests that while cars are involved in a wide range of accidents, including serious ones, other vehicle types tend to be less frequently associated with fatal outcomes. This could be because these vehicles operate at slower speeds, and drivers are generally better able to avoid dangerous situations or sustain fewer injuries in accidents.
